Jane Addams

The Mother of Social Work

Jane Addams - author, suffragette, and social work pioneer, 1912

A Timeline of Jane Addams' Life

1860
Born in Cedarville, Illinois.
1862
Her mother, Sarah Addams, dies.
1864
Contracts Pott's disease, leading to a curvature of the spine and lifelong health problems.
1868
Her father remarries, to Anna Hostetter Haldeman.
1881
Graduates Rockford Female Seminary with a collegiate certificate and membership in Phi Beta Kappa. Inspired by her mother, she hopes to become a doctor so she can help the poor in Cedarville. Her father, John H. Addams, dies that summer. She moves to Philadelphia for a year to pursue a medical education, but due to her physical and mental health, does not complete a degree.
1883
After receiving spinal surgery from her brother-in-law, travels Europe with her stepmother, deciding to help the poor without becoming a doctor.
1887
Falls into depression over the conventional life expected of well-off women of her time. Visits the first settlement house - a home where rich and poor live together in an interdependent community - Toynbee Hall, in London. She dreams of opening one of her own.
1889
Co-founds Hull House, a settlement house in Chicago, with Ellen Gates Starr. It will eventually house 25 women and be visited by more than 2,000 people weekly.
1894
Is the first woman to be appointed sanitary inspector to Chicago's 19th ward. Under her leadership, over a thousand new health violations are reported, reducing disease and death in the ward.
1898
Joins the Anti-Imperialist League against the annexation of the Philippines.
1905
Is a charter member of the American Sociological Society. During her lifetime, she will remain the most prominent female memeber.
1909
Argues that play and recreational programs are critical for children.
1910
Helps mediate during the Garment Worker's Strike.
1912
Helps form the new Progressive Party and supports Theodore Roosevelt's presidential campaign.
1915
Becomes a part of the Woman's Peace Party and is elected national chairman. She is invited to preside over the International Congress of Women at the Hague and is chosen to head the commission to find an end to the war. She is elected president of the International Committee of Women for a Permanent Peace; she will maintain her presidency through the organization being developed into the WILPFabbr>.
1931
Is the first woman awarded a Nobel Peace prize.
1935
Dies in Chicago, Illinois.

"What after all has maintained the human race on this old globe despite all the calamities of nature and all the tragic failings of mankind, if not faith in new possibilities and courage to advocate them."

Jane Addams
